Evaluating language models through the lens of human language acquisition requires developmentally grounded benchmarks that support direct comparisons between models and children. We introduce \meCDI{}, a count-based lexical benchmark that compares language model generation with child word production across age groups. \meCDI{} adapts the MacArthur-Bates Communicative Development Inventories (CDI) -- standardized parent-report checklists of early vocabulary -- by matching the size of training and generation sets and the word frequency distribution in the evaluation set. Using this benchmark, we evaluate character-level language models trained on a quantity of words equivalent to that of children. While models develop a larger vocabulary with more data, they diverge fundamentally from children's: models show excessive lexical diversity, persistently high non-word rates that fail to decline with exposure, and linear vocabulary growth that lacks children's typical vocabulary explosion. Moreover, model generations overrepresent high-frequency function words and underrepresent low-frequency content words.  These discrepancies cannot be addressed by temperature trade-offs during inference. Further frequency analysis of the evaluation set suggests that the mismatch might be linked to the more general phenomenon of long-tail truncation observed in language models, which makes them unable to learn words based on few-shot observations.
